# Brand and tone of voice: core best-practice styleguide

**Created:** 27/05/2026
**Purpose:** A brand-agnostic set of principles for brand positioning, tone of voice, visual identity, and writing style. Use as the default reference when establishing or refining a brand. Adapt the specifics, keep the principles.

---

## 1. Brand positioning

A good positioning statement does three things in plain language: it names who the brand is for, what it does that others do not, and the conviction behind the work. Avoid category language any competitor could honestly write. The strongest positioning is one a rival could not claim with a straight face.

Principles:

- Lead with the conviction, not the category. If the brand stands for something specific, say what
- Define the brand against what it is not. Contrast clarifies meaning
- Name the human stakes before the technical or commercial ones
- Anchor positioning in something the founders or the practice genuinely believe. Adopted positioning fades, held positioning endures.

---

## 2. Tone of voice

Tone is what people remember when they cannot remember the words. Treat it as a fixed set of choices, not a mood that drifts with the writer.

Principles:

- Expert but accessible. Talk to intelligent people who are not specialists. Explain ideas in terms of their implications, not their mechanics
- Human-first by default. Every line should ask what this means for the person reading, not for the system being described
- Considered and unhurried. No hype cycles, no urgency theatre. Match the pace at which good decisions are made
- Direct without being blunt. Say what you mean, in the manner of a wise friend who tells you the truth without making you feel stupid
- Intellectually warm. References to ideas, research, and real-world experience belong here. Carry confidence without arrogance
- Self-aware. Acknowledge complexity, resist false certainty, never over-promise.

---

## 3. Writing style

Style is the daily expression of tone. Most copy lives or dies on these choices.

Do:

- Use full sentences and considered phrasing. Get to the point without being clipped
- Use active voice. "We help organisations adopt X" not "X adoption is supported by us"
- Lead with the human and organisational stakes before the technical detail
- Keep humour dry and self-aware. Cheerful enthusiasm is rarely the right register
- Make testimonials and case studies specific. Vague outcomes are worse than no data.

Avoid:

- Hype vocabulary: "leverage", "unlock potential", "empower", "cutting-edge", "seamless", "disruptive". These are the words of organisations that have not thought hard enough
- Corporate filler: "stakeholder engagement", "value-add deliverables", "best-in-class"
- Formality for its own sake. Plain language outperforms formal language in almost every context
- Evangelical claims. Scepticism reads as honesty, certainty reads as marketing.

## 4. Visual identity: logo principles

The logo is the brand's signature. Treat it as a fixed asset, not a flexible one.

Principles:

- Set the logotype in one editorial typeface, and let one small detail carry the brand's signature (a coloured punctuation mark, a ligature, a weight shift). One detail, not three
- Define an abbreviated mark for constrained contexts such as favicons, app icons, and social thumbnails
- Set a minimum size for both forms. Below that size, the mark loses its character
- Define clear space as a function of the mark itself, typically one cap-height on all sides, so it scales correctly
- List acceptable backgrounds explicitly. Anything outside the list needs a defined treatment, such as a semi-transparent overlay.

Do not:

- Distort, rotate, or apply drop shadows to the mark
- Place the mark on photographic backgrounds without a defined overlay
- Render the mark in any colour outside the defined palette.

---

## 5. Visual identity: colour principles

Colour is structural, not decorative. The palette carries the brand's emotional register before the words do.

Principles:

- Choose a single accent colour. Use it for primary actions, active states, pull-quote borders, and key data highlights. The accent is singular by design
- Choose a surface range, not a single surface colour. Two or three closely related shades give depth without breaking unity
- Choose a primary text colour that sits well on the surface range. Off-white on dark surfaces reads warmer than pure white
- Choose a secondary text colour for metadata, captions, and supporting information
- Define hover and highlight states inside the existing palette. A new colour should be a brand decision, not a designer's preference.

Do not:

- Use the accent colour as a large background fill. It is an accent, not a surface
- Use pure white or pure black unless the palette is deliberately neutral. The warmth or coolness of off-shades carries meaning
- Introduce a second accent without a brand decision
- Mix tonal families (warm and cool) without intent.

---

## 6. Visual identity: typography principles

Typography sets the pace at which the brand is read.

Principles:

- Pair two typefaces: one editorial, one functional
- Use the editorial face for headings, display text, pull quotes, and section titles. Vary weight by size: lighter for large display, heavier for small display
- Use the functional face for body copy, UI labels, buttons, captions, and navigation
- Never use a display face below twenty pixels. It loses its character and becomes hard to read
- Never use the functional face for editorial headings that need to carry brand weight. They read as generic
- Reserve unusual letter spacing for the logotype and section dividers. Do not apply it to body text.

---

## 7. What the brand is not

Defining the boundary is as important as defining the centre. Every brand should be able to finish the sentence "we are not."

Principles for setting boundaries:

- Name what the brand sells, and what it does not. If it sells expertise, it does not sell software. Conflating these confuses buyers
- Name the philosophy the brand resists. If the field tends toward hype, the brand can be sceptical by default
- Name the register the brand will not use. If the field tends toward formality, the brand can be plain. If the field tends toward novelty, the brand can be considered
- Name the qualities the brand will not adopt even under pressure. Whatever is structural to the brand should be non-negotiable.

---

## 8. Applying this guide

This is a styleguide, not a template. The principles travel, the specifics do not.

When adapting to a specific brand:

- Replace the abstract category labels with the brand's own positioning
- Choose specific hex values, typefaces, and weights that fit the brand's register
- Add brand-specific do's and don'ts based on the field the brand competes in
- Pressure-test the result by asking: could a competitor honestly claim this? If yes, the brand has not gone far enough.
