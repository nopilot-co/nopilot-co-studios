Where adoption has stalled

Most businesses have tried something. Few have changed much.

The common pattern is a pilot that impressed in the room and then went nowhere. The reasons repeat across sectors:

  • Twenty systems of record, and no single system of truth.
  • Employees bringing ChatGPT to work, while IT brings a firewall.
  • A transformation effort handed to a team that cannot change how the business actually runs.

We have seen where this ends up more than once: a company that tried, gained 17 new logins and three subscriptions, and made no real progress.

What we think is coming

The next two to three years tighten things in a few likely ways:

  • Regulation tightens. The EU AI Act's transparency duties apply from 2 August 2026. The heavier high-risk obligations were due the same day, and are now proposed to move to 2 December 2027, so the exact deadline is still in motion. Either way the direction is one way, and most mid-market firms are not ready. Readiness is becoming a condition of doing business rather than a nice-to-have. We aim to pair every claim we make about AI with the governance and oversight that backs it.
  • Running costs shift. The cost of operating these systems will move, and budgeting for it is still guesswork in most firms.
  • Customer expectations keep climbing. What felt advanced last year is the baseline now, and customers do not migrate gently. They leapfrog.
  • Traditional industry models come under pressure. Whole workflows, and some whole roles, get rebuilt around what these systems can now do.
  • Software vendors feel it too. When an agent can assemble what a SaaS product used to sell, the per-seat model starts to look fragile. In time, the buyer of your business may be an agent acting for a competitor.

  • Leaders are the lever, not the IT department. Adoption that lasts starts with the people who set direction and own outcomes. It cannot be delegated away.

What an agentic business looks like

An agentic business is one where agents do real work, not demos, because the environment around them is built for it.

The research is fairly clear on why things fail, and it points the same way each time. MIT's GenAI Divide study, built on 150 leader interviews, a 350-person survey, and 300 public deployments, puts it plainly: "the core barrier to scaling is not infrastructure, regulation, or talent. It is learning." Most systems do not retain feedback, adapt to context, or improve over time. (web-verified, proof-market.md B2; )

The research community frames the same finding as an environment problem rather than an intelligence problem. Agents drift on long tasks, decay on messy data, and loop on open-ended ones. They do better when the environment is well defined, with rules, constraints, and trusted memory. (web-verified, Stuart Winter-Tear, November 2025, proof-market.md B2)

So the picture is fairly concrete. An agentic business has a single account of itself that the work can draw on, rather than 20 systems of record and no system of truth. Its knowledge, processes, and decisions are written down, so an agent inherits them instead of guessing. Its agents are kept on rails, with a person owning the outcome and the oversight behind it. This is the version we aim to build, and we think it is the version the evidence supports.

It is worth saying what it is not. One documented attempt to run a startup on AI agents alone produced hallucinated updates, invented campaigns, and agents that, in the researcher's phrase, "talked themselves to death" on circular conversation. Coordination between agents is still fragile, and unconstrained autonomy is a liability. (web-verified, Stuart Winter-Tear citing BCG, November 2025, proof-market.md B3) The honest claim is the governed, human-led one. Every capability we describe comes with the oversight that makes it safe.

"You're not hiring a system anymore, you're hiring an agent. Would you grab a random person off the street and just say, here's my black book?" Dan Rawles

How long the window stays open

The honest answer is that nobody can date this precisely, and we should not pretend to. BCG itself says it does not know whether AI-only firms are five or 15 years away. (web-verified, proof-market.md B3) What we can read with more confidence is the slope, and the slope is steep.

Adoption is already near-universal, while value is still rare. McKinsey's November 2025 State of AI finds 88% of organisations now use AI in at least one function, up from 78% a year earlier, but only 39% report enterprise-level profit impact, and just 6% qualify as high performers with 5% or more of profit attributable to AI. On agents specifically, 23% of organisations are scaling an agentic system somewhere and a further 39% are experimenting, so 62% are at least in motion. (web-verified, proof-market.md B1; )

The UK mid-market sits on the same curve. AI use there rose from about 35% in 2023 to 55% by the end of 2025, yet only 24% are productive adopters. (web-verified, HSBC, proof-market.md C2) The gap between using AI and using it well is the opportunity, and it is closing. Once the productive minority becomes the productive majority, the firms still circling are not behind on a tool. They are behind on how their market works.

This is why we treat the window as a parameter, not a slogan. The chance to help a firm cross that gap is real now and time-limited. It does not need urgency theatre. The numbers carry it.

"It's a bit like a headlight in the eyes. At the moment it's 'I've got time to do this, it's never going to happen', to 'oh, it's happening'. There isn't any more luxury in terms of planning it all out." Dan Rawles

The adoption strategies that work

The same evidence that explains why pilots stall also shows what the firms on the right side of the divide do differently. There are three moves, and they are not exotic.

The first is to buy and partner rather than build alone. MIT found that bought-in specialist tools and partnerships succeed about 67% of the time, against roughly a third of that for internal builds. (web-verified, proof-adoption.md (a)) The willing firm that tries to do this with internal effort alone is the one most likely to end up with 17 logins and no progress.

The second is to move from piloting to scaled ways of working, on purpose. MIT CISR's survey of 152 firms found the greatest financial impact comes from the step out of pilots and into scaled operation. Firms stuck at the pilot stage perform below their industry average; firms that scale perform well above it. (web-verified, MIT CISR, August 2025, proof-market.md B2) The pilot is not the win. Crossing out of it is.

The third is to start with leaders, not the IT department. The named barriers in UK surveys are lack of in-house expertise at 35%, cost at 30%, and uncertainty on return at 25%, with deeper causes in weak governance and the absence of a credible internal champion. (web-verified, techUK, proof-market.md C2) Adoption that lasts is owned by someone with the authority to change how the business runs.

Method

The landscape section argued that context is the scarce thing and that leaders are the lever. This section is about how we might turn that into a business that grows beyond the two of us.

The shape is one idea. We hope to run a hybrid consulting platform. Our own AI agents would do the repeatable work and our consultants would do the judgement work. Three things follow from that shape, and they are the whole method:

1. A lean core that we keep deliberately small, with delivery added through a partner network rather than a payroll.

A lean core, a partner edge

The core stays small on purpose. Two founders, plus a small fractional team for delivery, finance operations, and technical operations. No employees, no offices, no fixed assets to carry through a quiet month.

Capacity beyond the core would come from a delivery network of contractors and consulting partners, engaged on framework-driven agreements rather than salaries. They deliver our method, to our standard, on our governance. We would add partners when the work is there, rather than pay for capacity when it is not.

We think this keeps the cost base honest through the build. It also tries to hold to the first parameter you set: scale by adding capacity beyond us, not by us working more hours.

A delivery framework that grows sideways

The framework is a set of repeatable units of work: a discovery, a pilot, a retainer. Each one is a defined shape we can run again for the next client. We would not rebuild it per engagement. We would repeat it, refine it, and train partners to extend it.

Growth would then be both horizontal and vertical. The same units run across more clients, in more sectors, through more partners, with the platform underneath getting a little sharper each time. We are not betting the business on a single product release that has to land. We are trying to extend a method, one client at a time.

We think this protects the numbers. The operation would scale with the business we are building, not with how big a platform we can afford to build first. The platform comes, but in support of growth, not driving it. The idea is that the platform does the scaling that headcount would otherwise have to.

Technology platform

The dartboard

The method needs a stack to run on, and the stack needs a shape that is easy to hold in mind. We draw it as a dartboard: four rings around a single centre.

The centre is the most personal layer, and the easiest to overlook. Each ring out from it is more shared, more standard, and closer to the systems a client already owns. The further out you go, the less of it we build. We tend to manage or simply recommend it instead. Nothing in the outer rings is ours to keep. The client owns their systems of record and their infrastructure. We own the method, and the layer that makes the rest legible to itself.

Read the rings from the inside out. That order is deliberate. It is the order in which capability builds, and the order in which a client adopts it.

The four rings, in one line each:

  • Bullseye: the personal context layer, one person or small team made legible to their own agents.
  • Inner ring: the same idea at scale, repeatable context and code units behind one interface and one login.
  • Middle ring: the client's own systems of record, reached through a managed integration layer.
  • Outer ring: a short, named list of infrastructure pillars, owned by the client or managed by us for a fee.

What follows is one panel per ring.

bullseye, the personal context layer

The smallest unit of the whole stack is one person working well with their own agents. We call it a personal Business Operating System (PBOS): a context layer for an individual or a small team, before any of it scales.

In practice it is plain. A set of context files the person controls, held on their own device or in their own cloud. An index over those files, so an agent can retrieve the right passage at the right moment, built on open components such as pgvector. A small knowledge graph that ties the pieces into one source of truth, rather than twenty scattered notes. And an activation layer the person actually uses day to day, whether that is a Claude plugin or a personal assistant tool. Every retrieval and every action stays under the person's own oversight. A context layer the user cannot see into is one they will not trust.

This is the proof in the document you are reading. It was written with exactly this layer underneath it: my own context, my own index, an agent working as a third hand under my direction. We are not describing something we hope to build. We are describing the thing we already run on.

The bullseye matters because adoption that lasts starts with a person, not a platform. Get one leader legible to their own agents, and you have the seed of everything in the rings around it.

inner ring, scaled context and code

The inner ring is the bullseye made repeatable. The same idea, context plus an agent, built once as a unit of work and run again for the next person, the next team, the next client.

These units do four kinds of encoding: content, data, knowledge, and process. Each one turns something the business knows but cannot yet act on into something an agent can. The units are built in Python with open-source tooling, rather than a single bought product. That is what keeps them cheap to run and quick to adapt. They sit behind one interface and one login, so a client meets a single coherent system rather than a drawer of disconnected scripts.

This is the part we have built before, at speed. Jules built a pipeline that reverse-engineers a design system from an existing site, tokenising its styles, generating brand guidelines and a governance suite and performance targets, then standing up a headless front end from plain YAML specifications. He used it to rebuild five brands, with full content migration and copy transformation, in about a day and a half. (local-source: conversation-2026-04-16) That is the inner ring working: a repeatable unit, governed, run again and again, the platform getting a little sharper each time.

The economics of this ring are what make the model hold. In the base case, the cost of the AI and platform layer stays under 5% of revenue, at about 3.5% by Year 3, because the agent-executed work runs at roughly £8 an hour against a loaded human cost of about £85.

middle ring, the client's systems of record

The middle ring is where our stack meets the client's. It is their systems of record: the customer platform, the resource and finance systems, the workspace, the production tools. Twenty of them, usually, with no single system of truth across the lot. That gap is the problem the whole method exists to close.

We do not replace these systems, and we do not ask the client to migrate off them. We reach them through a managed integration layer: a provided set of connections that lets the context layer read from and write to the systems the business already runs on. The aim is to make the existing estate legible to itself, not to add a twenty-first login to the pile.

This is governed by design. Every connection is scoped, every read and write is accountable, and the client can see what the layer touches and why. An integration layer that quietly reaches everywhere is the kind of thing a senior buyer is right to refuse.

outer ring, the infrastructure pillars

The outer ring is the ground the rest sits on: a short, named list of infrastructure providers rather than an open field. We recommend a select set of pillars, GCP, AWS, Azure, Hetzner, Supabase, and Sanity, and we stop there. Fewer options, deliberately, because a recommended stack we know well is cheaper to run and safer to govern than one assembled fresh per client.

There are two ways a client can hold this ring, and the choice is theirs. Either they own the infrastructure directly, on their own accounts, and we operate within it. Or we manage it for them as a service, on a clear fee above cost, with on-premise arrangements where a client needs them. Either way the client owns the systems and the data. We never hold a client's estate hostage to our involvement, which is the same ownership promise that runs through every layer above.

We have shipped real work on these pillars. Dan delivered agentic content-managed sites for HSS Hire, a tool-hire group at genuine PLC scale, built on Sanity, alongside a personalisation campaign that adapted creative to each customer's own neighbourhood, something that "was never possible because it was too expensive" before. (local-source: twinmind-2026-04-15) The pillars are not a wish list. They are where work already runs.

The dartboard is one picture with one rule running through it. The closer to the centre, the more we build and the more personal it is. The closer to the edge, the more the client owns and the more we simply manage or recommend. Capability builds from the bullseye out, and ownership stays with the client at every ring.

  • A delivery team brought in against framework agreements, at contractor rates, as the work demands.

The company carries zero employees, zero physical assets, and zero creditors. That is what keeps it light, and what keeps the option to step back or sell open from day one. The financial model holds the same shape: headcount of one at the end of year one, rising to roughly 9.5 by the end of year three, almost all of it fractional or framework-based rather than salaried.
