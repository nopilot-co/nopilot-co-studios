Commercial AI reshaped the everything in roughtly two years. ### What we know
The hype cycle ran quickly and spread across LinkedIn like wildfire. Early excitement, a rush of pilots, then a quieter period as the hard parts showed themselves: messy data, unclear ownership, and tools that demonstrated well but changed little. Boards got excited, many made headcount bets on the back of early predictions. 

But for many, the capability arrived faster than the ability to absorb it. The gap between the two is where most of the difficulty now sits - particularly for mid-market organsiations that can't fritter away enterprise R&D investments.

A few firms pulled ahead. Most are still circling. 

> ** 93% ** of pilots failed to progress [[PROOF: MIT]]

> ** ~80%  ** of organisations planning pilots [[PROOF: MIT]]
### Where adoption has stalled

Most businesses have tried something. Few have changed much.

The common pattern is a pilot that impressed in the room and then went nowhere. The reasons repeat across sectors:

- Twenty systems of record, and no single system of truth.
- Employees bringing ChatGPT to work, while IT brings a firewall.
- A transformation effort delegated to a team that cannot change how the business actually runs.

We have seen the end state of this more than once: a company that tried, ended up with 17 new logins and three subscriptions, and made no real progress. *[[PROOF: named UK mid-market examples of stalled adoption, with the specific pitfall in each case. Source and confirm shareability.]]*
### What we think is coming

The next two to three years tighten the screws in a few predictable ways:

- **Regulation tightens.** The EU AI Act's transparency duties apply from 2 August 2026. The heavier high-risk obligations were due the same day, and are now proposed to move to 2 December 2027, so the exact deadline is still in motion. Either way the direction is one way, and most mid-market firms are not ready. Readiness is becoming a condition of doing business, not a nice-to-have. Every claim we make about AI we will pair with the governance and oversight that backs it. *[[validate: high-risk delay to 2 Dec 2027 is provisional, per the 7 May 2026 agreement, not yet adopted; confirm UK direction.]]*
- **Running costs shift.** The economics of operating these systems will move, and budgeting for them is still guesswork in most firms. *[[validate: the scaffold assumes token costs rise; confirm the direction, since some forecasts point the other way.]]*
- **Customer expectations keep climbing.** What felt advanced last year is the baseline now, and customers do not migrate gently. They leapfrog.
- **Traditional industry models come under pressure.** Whole workflows, and some whole roles, get rebuilt around what these systems can now do.
- **Software vendors feel it too.** When an agent can assemble what a SaaS product used to sell, the per-seat model starts to look fragile. In time, the buyer of your business may be an agent acting for a competitor.
### What we need to know

Underneath all of it sit two conclusions that shape everything we propose:

- **Business context is the thing that separates the firms that thrive from the firms that stall.** Tools are everywhere and cheap. Context is rare and hard-won. The firms that win make their business legible to itself, so the right work reaches the right place with the right knowledge attached.
- **Leaders are the lever, not the IT department.** Adoption that lasts starts with the people who set direction and own outcomes. It cannot be delegated away.

This is the ground our model stands on. The how, our Business Operating System (BOS), and what it means for you, comes next.

## Vision {#vision}

### Where this is going

The Landscape said tools are cheap and context is rare. The Vision is the simple consequence of that. The businesses that win the next few years will not be the ones with the best models. They will be the ones whose own operations are legible enough for agents to work inside them, under human direction. That is what we mean by an agentic business.

We are not describing a company run by AI. The story above gives a more nuanced conclusion. We are describing a company that has made itself understandable to the software acting on its behalf, so the right work reaches the right place with the right knowledge attached, and a person stays accountable for the result. This is about getting the balance right, and that takes expertise and teh ability to listen before acting.

Three things follow, and they are the spine of this section. First, what an agentic business actually looks like, said plainly. Second, how long the window stays open before this is simply how mid-market firms operate. Third, the adoption strategies the evidence says actually work, as opposed to the ones that stall. Our purpose, which comes next, falls straight out of the third.

---

### What an agentic business looks like

Strip away the noise and an agentic business is one where agents do real work, not demos, because the environment around them is built for it.

The research is unusually clear on the cause of failure, and it points the same way every time. MIT's GenAI Divide study, built on 150 leader interviews, a 350-person survey, and 300 public deployments, puts it directly: "the core barrier to scaling is not infrastructure, regulation, or talent. It is learning." Most systems do not retain feedback, adapt to context, or improve over time. *(web-verified, proof-market.md B2; [[validate: confirm the MIT 95% and the quoted line against the original MIT report, not secondary coverage]])*

The research community frames the same finding as an environment problem, not an intelligence problem. Agents drift on long tasks, decay on messy data, and loop on open-ended ones. Performance jumps when the environment is well defined, with rules, constraints, and trusted memory. *(web-verified, Stuart Winter-Tear, November 2025, proof-market.md B2)*

So the picture is concrete. An agentic business has a single account of itself that the work can draw on, rather than 20 systems of record and no system of truth. Its knowledge, processes, and decisions are encoded, so an agent inherits them instead of guessing. Its agents are kept on rails, with a person owning the outcome and the oversight that backs it. This is the version we build, and it is the version the evidence supports.

It is worth saying what it is not. A documented attempt to run a startup on AI agents alone produced hallucinated updates, invented campaigns, and agents that, in the researcher's phrase, "talked themselves to death" on circular conversation. Coordination between agents is still brittle and unconstrained autonomy is a liability. *(web-verified, Stuart Winter-Tear citing BCG, November 2025, proof-market.md B3)* The honest claim is the governed, human-led one. Every capability we describe comes paired with the oversight that makes it safe.

> "You're not hiring a system anymore, you're hiring an agent. Would you grab a random person off the street and just say, here's my black book?"
> Dan Rawles

---

### How long the window stays open

The honest answer is that nobody can date this precisely, and we should not pretend to. BCG itself admits it does not know whether AI-only firms are five or 15 years away. *(web-verified, proof-market.md B3)* What we can read with more confidence is the slope, and the slope is steep.

Adoption is already near-universal, while value is still rare. McKinsey's November 2025 State of AI finds 88% of organisations now use AI in at least one function, up from 78% a year earlier, but only 39% report enterprise-level profit impact, and just 6% qualify as high performers with 5% or more of profit attributable to AI. On agents specifically, 23% of organisations are scaling an agentic system somewhere and a further 39% are experimenting, so 62% are at least in motion. *(web-verified, proof-market.md B1; [[validate: confirm the McKinsey 6% / 88% / 39% figures against the primary November 2025 PDF]])*

The UK mid-market sits on the same curve. AI use there climbed from about 35% in 2023 to 55% by the end of 2025, yet only 24% are productive adopters. *(web-verified, HSBC, proof-market.md C2)* The gap between using AI and using it well is the whole opportunity, and it is closing. Once the productive minority becomes the productive majority, the firms still circling are not behind on a tool. They are behind on how their market works.

This is why we hold the window as a parameter, not a slogan. The chance to help a firm cross that gap is real now and time-limited. It does not need urgency theatre. The numbers carry it.

> "It's a bit like a headlight in the eyes. At the moment it's 'I've got time to do this, it's never going to happen', to 'oh, it's happening'. There isn't any more luxury in terms of planning it all out."
> Dan Rawles

### The adoption strategies that work

The same body of evidence that explains why pilots stall also shows what the firms on the right side of the divide do differently. There are three moves, and they are not exotic.

The first is to buy and partner rather than build alone. MIT found that bought-in specialist tools and partnerships succeed about 67% of the time, against roughly a third of that for internal builds. *(web-verified, proof-adoption.md (a))* The willing firm that tries to do this with internal effort alone is the firm most likely to end up with 17 logins and no progress.

The second is to move from piloting to scaled ways of working, deliberately. MIT CISR's survey of 152 firms found the greatest financial impact comes from the step out of pilots and into scaled operation. Firms stuck at the pilot stage perform below their industry average; firms that scale perform well above it. *(web-verified, MIT CISR, August 2025, proof-market.md B2)* The pilot is not the win. Crossing out of it is.

The third is to start with leaders, not the IT department. The named barriers in UK surveys are lack of in-house expertise at 35%, cost at 30%, and uncertainty on return at 25%, with deeper causes in weak governance and the absence of a credible internal champion. *(web-verified, techUK, proof-market.md C2)* Adoption that lasts is owned by someone with the authority to change how the business runs.

This is the part we have proof on, because it is what we already do. We have built the small, cheaper, less disruptive thing that displaced a six-figure platform decision, taken a support team from 12 people to three, and rebuilt five brands in about a day and a half on our own method. *(local-source, proof-adoption.md (b); founder figures validated.)* We run on what we describe.

> "Not building something which is going to stay in the boardroom, but it's born in the boardroom, it's got ownership in the boardroom, and then it flows out from there."
> Jules

### Our purpose in helping

The third strategy is where we live, and it is the bridge to the next section.

The firms that need this most are the willing majority. They have bought tools, run a pilot, watched it stall, and lack the in-house expertise and governance to scale it. They are not short of ambition. They are short of a way through that they can own. Our purpose is to give them that way through, and to leave the capability with them rather than holding it hostage.

That means three commitments, stated here and carried into the Purpose section. We help leadership teams compete, evolve, and grow through agentic adoption that is genuinely their own. We build the operating context with them, not around them, so the knowledge stays in the business. And we keep every claim paired with the oversight that makes it safe, because a firm presenting itself as agentic will be scrutinised harder than most, and so will we.

> "First we find leaders with budgets who are keen and open and feel the need to learn this technology, so they have the credibility, the authority and the understanding to project it out onto their organisation."
> Jules

This is the vision in one line. An agentic business is a business made legible enough for agents to work in it safely, under human direction. The window to help mid-market firms become one is open now and closing. Our purpose, next, is how we choose to spend it.

## Method {#method}

### How the business actually works

The landscape says context is the scarce thing and leaders are the lever. The method is how we turn that into a business that grows past the two of us.

The shape is one idea. We run a hybrid consulting platform: our own AI agents do the repeatable work, our consultants do the judgement work, and the two are designed to fit together rather than compete. The agents carry load. The people carry the room. That split is what lets us serve more clients without adding people at the same rate, and it is the difference between a practice and a business.

Three things follow from that shape, and they are the whole method:

>! 1. A lean core that we keep deliberately small, with delivery added through a partner network rather than a payroll.

>! 2. A framework that grows sideways, by repeating proven units of work across more clients, not by building a bigger product.

>! 3. A commercial model that earns from the value the client gains, so our growth depends on theirs.

We do not just advise on this. We run on it. This document is the first small proof: written with AI as a working third hand, under our direction, with us accountable for every line. We offer a ** consulting service ** to help you change, and a ** technology platform ** to help you bake it in.

### A lean core, a partner edge
The core stays small on purpose. Two founders, plus a tight fractional team for delivery, finance operations, and technical operations. No employees, no offices, no fixed assets to carry through a quiet month. Minimum aggravation.

Capacity beyond the core comes from a delivery network of contractors and consulting partners, engaged on framework-driven agreements rather than salaries. They deliver our method, to our standard, on our governance. We grow capacity by adding partners when the work is there, and we are not paying for capacity when it is not.

This is what keeps the cost base honest through the build. It is also what protects the first parameter you set: scale by adding capacity beyond us, not by us working more hours.

### A delivery framework that grows sideways
The framework is a set of repeatable units of work: a discovery, a pilot, a retainer, each one a defined shape we can run again for the next client. We do not rebuild it per engagement. We repeat it and refine it, we train partners to extend it.

Growth is therefore horizontal and vertical. The same units run across more clients, in more sectors, through more partners, with the platform underneath getting a little sharper each time. We are not betting the business on a single product release that has to land. We are extending a method that already works, one client at a time.

This protects the numbers: the operation scales with the business we are building, not with how big a platform we can afford to build first. The paltform comes - but in support of growth - not driving it. In the base case, revenue per person stays close to flat as the business grows, around £470k in Year 1 and a similar figure by Year 3, even as the team goes from one to about nine and a half and revenue goes from £470k to £4.52m. The platform does the scaling that headcount would otherwise have to. *[[modelled: revenue-per-FTE and headcount figures are from the v2 base case]]*

### Master: the spine

The model rests on four ideas. Read in order, they explain how a business adopts agentic AI in a way that lasts, and how that adoption pays us back.

The first two are about the client. Adoption starts with understanding, and understanding starts with leaders, not with workflow tinkering. Adoption then lasts only when that understanding is given structure: a Business Operating System (BOS) that holds the client's context, code, and encoded knowledge in one place.

## Our approach {#approach}

### Five steps, one direction

The work runs in five steps. They are sequential, but they are not a one-off project. Each step leaves the business better able to run the next one itself, so by the end the client owns the engine, not just the output.

The shape is simple. We collect what the business already knows, then formalise it into context: a set of distributed contexts unified by a shared knowledge graph into one system of truth. This is the answer to the failure pattern in the landscape. The MIT NANDA study puts about 95% of enterprise pilots at no measurable profit impact, and names the cause as tools that do not learn the organisation's workflow or context. *(web-verified: MIT NANDA, "The GenAI Divide", 2025)* Our approach starts where that failure starts, with the context, and builds outward from there.

Two things hold across all five steps. The client owns what we build with them. And every step where an agent acts is paired with the human oversight that governs it.

---

### 1. Collect

We start by gathering what the business already holds: how it works, what it knows, and the data it runs on.

Most of this exists already, scattered. It sits in twenty systems of record, in spreadsheets, in the heads of the people who have been there longest. The problem is rarely a shortage of knowledge. It is that the knowledge is not in one place, and no agent or newcomer can reach it.

We lead this step with the leadership team, because adoption that lasts begins with the people who set direction and own outcomes, not with the IT department. The output is an honest picture of where the knowledge lives and where the gaps are.

---

### 2. Formalise into context

We turn what we collected into context the business can act on.

That means distributed contexts, one per domain or team, unified by a shared knowledge graph into a single system of truth. Twenty systems of record become one place the business can reason from. Where there were 17 versions of the truth, there is one.

This is the step the stalled pilots skipped. Without retained, structured context, a capable model still has nothing stable to work in. With it, the business becomes legible to itself, so the right work reaches the right place with the right knowledge attached.

---

### 3. Operationalise

We make the context usable in the daily run of the business.

Context that sits in a document changes nothing. So we make it accessible, so people and agents can reach it where they work; actionable, so it drives real decisions; and present across the workflows that matter, not parked in one tool. It is built to improve as it is used, with each correction feeding back in.

Every point where an agent acts on this context is governed. A human sets the bounds, reviews the output, and stays accountable for it. Agents serve the people who make the decisions; they do not replace them.

---

### 4. Enable and extend

We train the people, roll the system out, and refine it with real use.

The aim is owned capability, human and agentic. The leadership team and the wider business gain the skill to run and improve the system, not a dependence on us to do it for them. This is what keeps the client in the driver's seat: when we step back, the knowledge stays, because it is encoded and because the people hold it.

We extend rather than replace. The honest, cheaper path is usually to fit the system to how people already work, not to make them migrate and retrain around a new platform. ### 5. Create the skunkworks

We stand up a small internal team that can keep innovating without us.

Skunkworks is a working label. *[[validate: confirm the internal-team name before any external use]]* The intent is plain: a lean group inside the client, trained and equipped to find the next opportunity, build it, and govern it, on the system they now own. This is the difference between a one-off transformation and a business that keeps adapting.

### Brand strategy

The brand has one job before the first meeting. It tells the right buyer this is for them, and the wrong buyer that it is not.

Three rules follow from that, and they hold whatever name we land on.

## Technology platform {#tech}

### The dartboard

The method needs a stack to run on, and the stack needs a shape you can hold in your head. We draw it as a dartboard: four rings around a single centre.

The centre is the most personal layer and the easiest to overlook. Each ring out from it is more shared, more standard, and closer to the systems a client already owns. The further out you go, the less of it we build and the more of it we manage or simply recommend. Nothing in the outer rings is ours to keep. The client owns their systems of record and their infrastructure. We own the method and the layer that makes the rest legible to itself.

Read the rings from the inside out. That order is deliberate: it is the order in which capability compounds, and the order in which a client adopts it.

[render: the dartboard graphic renders on its own diagram panel (see diagram_panels in the manifest); a horizontal four-ring detail row follows, one panel per ring, in the same inside-out order.]

The four rings, in one line each:

- Bullseye: the personal context layer, a single person or small team made legible to their own agents.
- Inner ring: the same idea at scale, repeatable context and code units behind one interface and one login.
- Middle ring: the client's own systems of record, reached through a managed integration layer.
- Outer ring: a short, named list of infrastructure pillars, owned by the client or managed by us for a fee.

What follows is one panel per ring.

---

### bullseye, the personal context layer

The smallest unit of the whole stack is one person working well with their own agents. We call it a personal Business Operating System (PBOS): a context layer for an individual or a small team, before any of it scales.

In practice it is plain. A set of context files the person controls, held on their own device or in their own cloud. An index over those files so an agent can retrieve the right passage at the right moment, built on open components such as pgvector. A small knowledge graph that ties the pieces into one source of truth rather than twenty scattered notes. And an activation layer the person actually uses day to day, whether that is a Claude plugin or a personal assistant tool. Every retrieval and every action stays under the person's own oversight, because a context layer the user cannot see into is one they will not trust.

This is the proof in the document you are reading. It was written with exactly this layer underneath it: my own context, my own index, an agent working as a third hand under my direction. We are not describing something we hope to build. We are describing the thing we already run on. *[[validate: confirm the PBOS components named here, pgvector index plus knowledge graph plus Claude plugin or assistant, match the current internal setup before this is shown.]]*

The bullseye matters because adoption that lasts starts with a person, not a platform. Get one leader legible to their own agents and you have the seed of everything in the rings around it.

---

### inner ring, scaled context and code

The inner ring is the bullseye made repeatable. The same idea, context plus an agent, built once as a unit of work and run again for the next person, the next team, the next client.

These units do four kinds of encoding: content, data, knowledge, and process. Each one turns something the business knows but cannot yet act on into something an agent can. The units are built in Python with open-source tooling rather than a single bought product, which is what keeps them cheap to run and quick to adapt. They sit behind one interface and one login, so a client meets a single coherent system rather than a drawer of disconnected scripts.

This is the part we have built before, at speed. Jules built a pipeline that reverse-engineers a design system from an existing site, tokenising its styles, generating brand guidelines and a governance suite and performance targets, then standing up a headless front end from plain YAML specifications. He used it to rebuild five brands, with full content migration and copy transformation, in about a day and a half. *(local-source: conversation-2026-04-16)* That is the inner ring working: a repeatable unit, governed, run again and again, the platform getting a little sharper each time.

The economics of this ring are what make the whole model hold. In the base case, the cost of the AI and platform layer stays under 5% of revenue, at about 3.5% by Year 3, because the agent-executed work runs at roughly £8 an hour against a loaded human cost of about £85. *[[modelled: 3.5% platform cost and the £8 versus £85 cost lines are from the v2 base case, not yet proven trading.]]*

---

### middle ring, the client's systems of record

The middle ring is where our stack meets the client's. It is their systems of record: the customer platform, the resource and finance systems, the workspace, the production tools. Twenty of them, usually, with no single system of truth across the lot. That gap is the problem the whole method exists to close.

We do not replace these systems and we do not ask the client to migrate off them. We reach them through a managed integration layer: a provided set of connections that lets the context layer read from and write to the systems the business already runs on. The point is to make the existing estate legible to itself, not to add a twenty-first login to the pile.

This is governed by design. Every connection is scoped, every read and write is accountable, and the client can see what the layer touches and why. An integration layer that quietly reaches everywhere is exactly the kind of thing a senior buyer is right to refuse. *[[validate: confirm whether the integration layer is built and operated by us, resold, or a mix, and how connection scopes are governed in practice.]]*

---

### outer ring, the infrastructure pillars

The outer ring is the ground the rest sits on: a short, named list of infrastructure providers rather than an open field. We recommend a select set of pillars, GCP, AWS, Azure, Hetzner, Supabase, and Sanity, and we stop there. Fewer options, deliberately, because a recommended stack we know well is cheaper to run and safer to govern than one assembled fresh per client.

There are two ways a client can hold this ring, and the choice is theirs. Either they own the infrastructure directly, on their own accounts, and we operate within it. Or we manage it for them as a service, on a clear fee above cost, with on-premise arrangements where a client needs them. Either way the client owns the systems and the data. We never hold a client's estate hostage to our involvement, which is the same ownership promise that runs through every layer above.

We have shipped real work on these pillars. Dan delivered agentic content-managed sites for HSS Hire, a tool-hire group at genuine PLC scale, built on Sanity, alongside a personalisation campaign that adapted creative to each customer's own neighbourhood, something that "was never possible because it was too expensive" before. *(local-source: twinmind-2026-04-15)* *[[validate: confirm shareability with Dan, and whether HSS or the Manchester agency is the nameable party.]]* The pillars are not a wish list. They are where work already runs.

The closer to the edge, the more the client owns and the more we simply manage or recommend. Capability compounds from the bullseye out, and ownership stays with the client at every ring.

### Built to be light, and easy to leave

The shape of the company is the proof that this is a business and not a job. We keep the legal entity simple, we hold our two stakes equal, and we keep the cost base light enough that the company can grow by adding capacity around us rather than by us working more hours.

The principle throughout is KISS: keep it simple. A UK private limited company. Two equal owners. No employees, no premises, no debt. Everything else hangs off framework agreements we can scale up or wind down.

The ramp runs in three stretches, which the swimlanes graphic sets out in full. From now to 1 September we land the first client. From 1 September to 1 January we operationalise the model. From 1 January to 1 April we ramp toward the targets. Each stretch has a clear job and a gate at the end of it.

The detail behind this sits in four cards: the legal entity, the ramp, the operational plan, and how we split the roles.

### The legal entity

We propose a UK private limited company with two equally weighted class-A director-shareholders: JRB Holdings and BereLucent. Equal shares, equal votes, equal dividends. The 50/50 line is clean and stays clean. No subsidiary, no majority holder, no outside backer. The company is ours, owned in two equal halves.

Founder pay starts at an equal day-rate, billed to the company at cost. At-cost means zero founder margin in the early months: £1 of founder-delivered revenue covers £1 of founder draw. Conserving cash this way is a deliberate, temporary bootstrap, not the model we settle into. For a £75k annual draw, the at-cost rate works out between £568 and £781 a day, depending on how many days each of us bills. The numbers behind that sit in the economics section.

Around the two of us we run two fractional layers, both on contractor terms, both scalable:

- A lean core team for delivery, finance operations, and technical operations.
- A delivery team brought in against framework agreements, at contractor rates, as the work demands.

The company carries zero employees, zero physical assets, and zero creditors. That is what keeps it light, and what keeps the option to step back or sell open from day one. The financial model holds the same shape: headcount of one at the end of year one, rising to roughly 9.5 by the end of year three, almost all of it fractional or framework-based rather than salaried. *[[validate: confirm JRB Holdings and BereLucent as the two holding entities, and that founder draw is set at £75k each at outset.]]*

### The ramp, now to April

The ramp runs in three stretches. The swimlanes graphic carries the detail; this is the spine.

This stretch is where the practice-to-business test is met or missed: growth has to come from added capacity, not from the two of us doing more.

### Brand name scoring: rubric and method

The brand naming section in Part five walks categories, finalists, and the head-to-head. This appendix reference page holds the full rubric workings so the decision track stays readable.

**Source files (in-repo mirror of context-message-360 brand-definition work):**

- `content/data/name-scoring.csv` — canonical 37-name table rendered in the scored shortlist panel (per-criterion scores and domain notes).
- `source/strategy/brand-naming/20260531-name-icp-scoring.csv` — extended scan with EUR domain pricing and shortlisted flags.

**Six criteria, scored 1–5, weighted to 100:**

| Criterion | Weight | What it tests |
|-----------|--------|---------------|
| Meaning | 25% | Does the name carry the business idea — context, clarity, connection — directly or by inference? |
| Register | 20% | Does it sound like a calm consultancy a board would trust, not a vendor or a product? |
| Distinctiveness | 20% | Is it ownable in conversation and search, or crowded by live firms? |
| Domain | 15% | Can we hold a credible primary URL at a sane price? |
| Travels | 10% | Does it work said aloud, in email, and in a one-line intro? |
| Platform | 10% | Does it leave room for a product or method name underneath without fighting the umbrella? |

**How to read the score.** `weighted_5` is the weighted average on the 1–5 scale; `score_100` is that average × 20. A high score is a reason to test a name, not a reason to lock it.
